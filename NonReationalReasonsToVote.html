The Complexity of Voting Decisions

We have seen that while rational calculation based on cost, benefit, and risk can explain some element of voting, there is much more to the story. Voters often cast their ballots for reasons beyond personal gain or risk. These motivations include (but are not limited to), loyalty to political parties, alignment with specific issues, and social influences. Understanding these additional factors provides a more comprehensive view of why individuals choose to vote.

Sample: types of Voters

Type of Voter Description
Party Loyalists These voters are dedicated to particular political parties, figures, or ideologies, often casting their vote consistently for one party, regardless of circumstances.
Issue-Based Voters These individuals vote based on specific issues they care about, such as abortion, climate change, or economic policy, rather than aligning with a party.
Peer-Influenced Voters Voters in this category are swayed by their social circles, peers, or communities. They may align their vote based on group consensus rather than personal preference.
The 'anyone but' voter These voters are those who may decide that anyone but the ruling party should get their vote. They may be seen as contrarians by nature, but a more friendly viewpoint could be that they wish to hold the incumbent government to account by seeking change.

You may recognise the patters above in friends, family, or other. These abstrations are not simple to measure or explain, but concepts in the social and biological sciences hold some answers to explain why things are the way they are.

As we move away from the utilitatian cost-benefit analysis suggested by the rational actor theory, we will explore the role of culture, biology, and the interaction between the two. Central to this discussion is a term previously used - heuristics.

Cognitive and subconscious processing

A heuristic isa cognitive shortcut or rule of thumb that people use to simplify decision-making and problem-solving processes. Heuristics allow individuals to make quick judgments and decisions without needing to analyse every piece of information in detail. While heuristics are often useful for efficiency, they can sometimes lead to biases or errors in reasoning.

For example, the availability heuristic refers to the tendency to judge the likelihood of an event based on how easily similar instances come to mind, which may not always provide an accurate representation of reality.

When approaching their decision to cast a ballot, an individual may associate the recent failings of the ruling party as indicative of the fact that they cannot be trusted to run a country. The information available to the individual is a recent failing, which leads them to consider future failings possible.

Consider some of the known, observable heuristics below:

Heuristic Description
Party Identification Heuristic Voters often rely on their alignment with a political party, using it as a shortcut to choose candidates without thoroughly analysing individual policies.
Endorsement Heuristic Voters may be influenced by endorsements from trusted groups, organisations, or individuals, assuming that the endorsement reflects a candidate's suitability.
Ideological Heuristic Voters use broad ideological positions (e.g., liberal or conservative) to make decisions without diving into specific policy details.
Name Recognition Heuristic Individuals may vote for candidates whose names they recognise, believing familiarity is a sign of credibility or effectiveness.
Polls Heuristic Some voters use opinion polls to decide, favouring candidates who are seen as more likely to win.
Retrospective Voting Heuristic Voters base their decisions on their perception of the performance of a party or candidate in office, judging whether they have benefited personally or seen positive outcomes during that time.

While heuristics can explain some of the ways in which individuals choose to vote, the picture is a little more complicated than we may expect. The above demonstrates a number of elements which will influence a voter's behaviour:

  • The information element refers to how much information is available to a voter. Memory, direct and indirect media consumption are key. The latter is important - a voter may be influenced by the presentation of particular candidates and parties sub-consciously, such as when more negative sentiment is used to describe them. This may not form a part of conscious media consumption but may have an effect on how a voter perceives things.
  • Heuristics are used consciously, but may be influenced by unconscious elements such as an individual's culture (an inhereted or culturally-learned heuristic), inherent biases, or even their natural tolerance for risk. Consider how people you know may approach challenges differently. Someone who has a low risk tolerance may perceive and process political information differently from someone who is highly risk tolerant.
  • The interaction between information and processing here emphasises that based on someone's cognitive processing, they may seek out specific information or news sources. In this sense, someone's way of thinking and feeling about the world may lead them to re-inforce specific ideas or conceptions, consciously or unconsciously.
  • Somewhat repetitive, but consider how on the day of voting these processes are still active and pushed to their limits. A new piece of information, learned close to an election, may have a larger impact on a voter since they have less time to think about it. Or perhaps their emotions on the day could swing an earlier decision. For instance if someone feels delicate and upset because they are unwell, will they still vote for a party who promises major cost savings from whittling away a national health service?

While the above is incomplete, it gives a taste of the sheer complexity involved in understanding what drives individual voters to cast their ballot for a particular candidate or party. A full model eludes social scientists, and even if one were to be developed, its impact may change the way voters behave in an interactive manner. Imagine if you were told by an algorithm how you will eventually vote - would that have a bearing on your decision to vote? Some individuals (like myself) may try to spite the social scientists by voting in a different manner (after all, we have already established that one vote is unlikely to make a difference)!
